Economics Careers: Understanding and Shaping the World’s Financial Systems

Economics is the study of how societies use resources, make decisions, and create policies that impact people and markets. Careers in economics combine analytical thinking with real-world impact, from shaping government policy to advising businesses on strategy.

Do You Have What It Takes?

If you enjoy analysing data, spotting patterns, and understanding the big picture of how economies function, a career in economics could be perfect for you. Economists are problem-solvers, critical thinkers, and skilled communicators who translate complex concepts into clear, actionable insights.

Attention to detail is crucial, as is curiosity—the best economists constantly question why trends occur and seek evidence-based answers. The work often involves collaboration with policymakers, business leaders, researchers, and international organisations.

Whether you’re passionate about public policy, financial markets, sustainability, or global development, economics offers a variety of career paths to suit your interests and skills.

Different Areas of Economics

  • Economic Policy – Advise governments on policies that affect the economy. Examples: UK Treasury, Bank of England, think tanks.
  • Data & Economic Analysis – Use statistics and models to analyse trends and predict future outcomes. Examples: Data analyst, economic forecaster, research economist.
  • International Economics & Trade – Study and advise on trade between countries and global markets. Examples: Trade policy adviser, international development economist.
  • Labour & Education Economics – Research employment, wages, and education systems. Examples: Labour market analyst, education policy adviser.
  • Environmental & Development Economics – Focus on sustainability, climate change, and economic growth in developing countries. Examples: Sustainability economist, development project officer.

UK Salary Ranges (2025)

  • Graduate economist / analyst: £28,000 – £35,000
  • Mid-level economist (3–5 years): £40,000 – £55,000
  • Senior economist / specialist: £55,000 – £80,000+
  • Head of economics / policy director: £80,000 – £120,000+

Government roles tend to be at the lower end, offering strong benefits and stability, while private sector roles in consulting and research often offer higher pay and faster career progression.

Routes into a Career in Economics

  • University Degree – Most economists have a degree in Economics or related subjects such as PPE, Business Economics, or International Relations with Economics. Strong maths skills are essential for quantitative roles.
  • Postgraduate Study – A Master’s or PhD in Economics can lead to specialist or high-level research positions.
  • Degree Apprenticeships – Some government departments and consultancies offer apprenticeships in data analysis, policy, or economics-related fields.
  • Graduate Schemes – Programmes such as the Civil Service Fast Stream, Bank of England graduate intake, and consultancy schemes offer structured training.

Career Progression

Graduate Economist / Analyst – Contribute to research projects, collect and interpret data, and produce reports.

Economist / Senior Analyst – Lead research, analyse findings, and present recommendations to decision-makers.

Senior Economist / Specialist – Manage teams, design economic models, and influence strategic decisions.

Head of Economics / Policy Director – Set strategic direction, oversee major economic initiatives, and work with senior government or business leaders.

Why Choose a Career in Economics?

  • Impact: Shape government policy, influence business strategies, and drive social change.
  • Transferable skills: Data analysis, research, and communication skills are valued across industries.
  • Variety: Opportunities exist in government, private firms, international organisations, and NGOs.
  • Good earning potential: Competitive salaries, especially in consulting and the private sector.
  • Global relevance: Economics knowledge is applicable worldwide.

Tips for Students Interested in Economics

  • Focus on maths, economics, and statistics for a strong academic foundation.
  • Stay informed by reading publications like The Economist or the Financial Times.
  • Build data skills—learn Excel, statistical software (R, Stata), and visualisation tools.
  • Seek internships in government, banks, or economic consultancies.
  • Stay curious and ask “why” to develop an analytical mindset.

Your Future in Economics

Economics careers offer the chance to combine analytical skills with meaningful real-world impact. Whether you want to shape national policy, help businesses make smarter decisions, or address global challenges like climate change, economics provides the tools to understand and improve the world we live in.
